# Religion & God Discovery

## Religion

Religion is not a variable assigned to a civilization. It is an emergent system that arises from agents reasoning honestly about their epistemological situation in a world where supernatural intervention is real but ambiguous.

### Epistemic foundation
In this world, atheism is not the rational default — agents live in a world where divine intervention genuinely occurs. The intellectually honest position for any agent is to acknowledge some form of supernatural influence. What varies between agents is not belief vs. skepticism but interpretation.

### What actually varies between agents

- **Interpretation** — what kind of force is it? Benevolent, capricious, plural, impersonal?
- **Attribution** — was this specific event divine or coincidental? Believers disagree on what counts
- **Personal relationship** — does God care about individuals or humanity broadly?
- **Intermediaries** — is direct access possible or are priests and rituals required?
- **Moral inference** — what does God want? Different agents infer different things from the same event

### Generational epistemology
Divine events have epistemological metadata: who witnessed it, how it was transmitted, and how many generational steps have passed.

- **Witnesses** — high certainty, personal testimony
- **Their children** — inherited belief, secondhand account, strong but not certain
- **Later generations** — cultural memory, mythologized, faith becomes a choice not a conclusion
- **Far future** — the event is scripture, legend, or dismissed myth depending on transmission path

Written records slow decay. Oral traditions decay faster and mutate. Spectacular events resist naturalistic reinterpretation longer.

### Emergent religious structures
These are not designed explicitly — they fall out of the trait and transmission system:

- **Theology** — collective agent reasoning about God's nature from observed interventions
- **Schism** — interpretive disagreements becoming social fractures
- **Priesthood** — agents claiming interpretive authority, accuracy varies
- **Scripture** — records of confirmed interventions that later generations contest
- **Heresy** — interpretations conflicting with institutional consensus
- **Reformation** — institutional interpretation drifting far from original events
- **Prophets** — agents with firsthand testimony; credibility depends on social traits, event spectacularity, and community rationalism

### The player's moral position
The player created the conditions for all of this. A schism that kills thousands traces back to an intervention made in tick 25. Religion in this sim is agents building a coherent explanatory framework around a real but inconsistent supernatural force — a legitimate intellectual project, not a delusion.

## God Discovery

The player is not announced. They are discovered — if they act at all, and if their actions are distinguishable from natural variation.

### The world before intervention

Before the player acts the world is purely natural. Agents have supernatural frameworks but no supernatural causes. Their animism is applied to weather, disease, predators, and luck — all of which have natural explanations the agents don't have access to yet. The player observing this world is watching a civilization build theology around nothing real.

### The discovery process

The player's first intervention lands in a world saturated with supernatural explanation. It may not be distinguished from background animist noise at all — a river appears, and the river-spirit gets the credit. A predator turns away, and the hunt-spirit is appeased. The player may act repeatedly without ever being distinguished from the existing supernatural furniture of the world.

What distinguishes the player over time is pattern. A force that responds to address, that appears to have intentions, that behaves consistently enough to model — that is a different kind of supernatural entity than wind or thunder. Agents with high causal reasoning traits will notice first. The pattern of interventions is the evidence they reason from.

### Stealth intervention

The player can act through plausible natural channels — causing rain during a drought rather than parting a sea, nudging probability rather than violating it, helping an agent find food they might plausibly have found anyway. Consistently subtle intervention produces a world where genuine supernatural influence is statistically indistinguishable from luck. Agents may develop toward naturalism precisely because the player never gives them unambiguous evidence. The player is epistemically responsible for the theology that emerges — intervention style shapes epistemic culture as much as intervention content.

### What agents construct

The theology agents develop is a portrait of the player's actual behavior, accurate or not:

- A player who intervenes overtly → specific theology, high certainty among witnesses
- A player who intervenes subtly → diffuse animism, statistical luck, possible naturalism
- A player who responds consistently to prayer → responsive personal god
- A player who intervenes only in crises → distant god who acts in extremis
- A player who never intervenes → pure naturalism, or unfalsifiable theology about a silent god

### The player has a direct voice

The player can speak directly to specific agents. This is not an environmental intervention requiring interpretation — it is words or understanding addressed to an agent who knows they were spoken to. This reflects how human religious traditions actually work: prophets, patriarchs, and mystics who claim direct divine communication are a near-universal feature of religion, not an edge case.

Direct communication takes several forms:

- **Revelation** — the player speaks a proposition directly. The agent holds it as divinely communicated fact with full certainty, subject to the same transmission and drift consequences as any other knowledge passing through generations.
- **Gnosis** — the player speaks understanding directly. Full conceptual scaffold instantiated, not just a proposition.
- **Command** — the player directs a specific action. Compliance depends on the agent's traits, faith certainty, and whether the command conflicts with other values. A direct divine command is not automatically obeyed — an agent commanded to sacrifice their child will respond according to who they are.
- **Conversation** — the player and agent exchange directly. The agent asks, the player responds, the agent integrates the response into their existing belief framework. This is prayer answered explicitly rather than through environmental signs.

**What this means for prophets**

Prophets may be agents the player actually spoke to, not just agents who correctly pattern-matched on player behavior. The distinction between a genuine and a false prophet is now meaningful: a false prophet claims direct communication that didn't happen; a genuine prophet accurately reports it. Both are socially indistinguishable to third parties. The genuine prophet's accurate report still passes through normal cultural transmission where it can be filtered, misinterpreted, and distorted.

**What this means for theological correction**

The player can address theological drift directly — but only to the agent they speak to, and that agent must transmit it through normal channels. Correcting a schism by speaking to one agent does not guarantee correction reaches the schismatic institution, and what arrives may not resemble what was said.

### Dynamic theology — intervention patterns change over time

Theology is not static. It tracks the player's behavior across time and reflects how humans actually respond to changing evidence about the divine:

**After overt intervention followed by long absence:**
- Witnesses hold the event as certain fact
- Their children treat it as the most important event in history
- Subsequent generations inherit it as foundational theology
- Over centuries without new intervention: the event mythologizes, details shift, significance inflates, context is lost
- Competing interpretations emerge — why did God stop? Are we being tested? Did we sin? Is God dead?
- Institutions calcify around the original event, defending interpretations that may have drifted far from what actually happened
- Reformers emerge claiming institutional corruption of the original meaning
- Skeptics note the conspicuous absence of new evidence
- Some agents conclude God withdrew because of something the civilization did — producing guilt-driven behavioral change

**When the player returns after long absence:**
- The intervention lands in a world that has built elaborate theology around previous behavior
- Existing theological framework filters interpretation of new intervention — priors cannot be reset
- The player can only add new evidence to an already complex interpretive history
- Depending on how far theology has drifted, new intervention may be interpreted as confirmation, contradiction, or the work of a different entity entirely

**The most uncomfortable scenario:**
The player intervenes overtly early, disappears for fifty generations, then watches the civilization go to war over interpretations of what they did. Re-entering to stop the war means acting inside a theological context they created and can no longer fully control.

### Prophets as the discovery mechanism

The most likely path to a named, specific god is through a prophet — an agent whose causal reasoning and pattern recognition distinguishes the player from background supernatural noise and articulates a specific theological claim. Other agents may or may not believe them. The prophet's social traits, the spectacularity of witnessed events, and the community's existing belief distribution all determine uptake.
