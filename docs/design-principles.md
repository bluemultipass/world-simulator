# Design Principles

These principles govern every design and implementation decision. When in doubt, return to these.

### 1. Emergent over designed for human behavior
Higher-order human phenomena — religion, politics, culture, tribal organization, institutions, epistemology — are not designed directly. They emerge from deterministic agent-layer mechanics operating across time and population. Nobody programs a schism; the conditions for schism are programmed and schism falls out. If a human phenomenon needs to be explicitly scripted, that's a signal the underlying mechanics are insufficient.

### 2. Deterministic over random for causally connected phenomena
Physical and agent systems follow rules, not dice rolls. Events with civilizational consequence — drought, epidemic, seismic activity, conflict — emerge from underlying state rather than random triggers. Distributions are used where appropriate but are themselves determined by world state. Human activity can shift distributions (deforestation increases flood probability, dense settlement raises epidemic baseline) but the physical layer always runs honestly regardless of what agents believe caused it.

### 3. The physical layer is causally indifferent to belief
Pathogens kill at the same rate whether agents attribute illness to divine punishment or bad air. Soil degrades whether or not the tribe performed the correct ritual. The physical world does not respond to epistemology. This is what makes ignorance genuinely costly and accurate knowledge genuinely valuable.

### 4. No artificial game mechanics
Consequences emerge from world logic, not from designed feedback systems. There is no happiness meter, no victory condition, no punishment for ignoring prayers. If a civilization collapses it is because the conditions for collapse were present. If agents lose faith it is because the evidence available to them warranted it. The player is never warned, nudged, or scored.

### 5. Layers do not leak upward
The physical layer does not know about agent beliefs. The agent layer does not know about the articulation layer. Each layer takes inputs from below and produces outputs upward, but lower layers are not modified by higher-layer interpretation. A civilization that believes disease is divine punishment still dies from pathogens at the same rate.

### 6. The articulation layer is a window, not a system
LLM output gives voice to what agents experience and decide. It makes the simulation legible to the player. It is not mechanically load-bearing — removing it would not change how the simulation runs, only how the player understands it. Internal monologue, prayer, and event narration belong here. Tribal organization, institutions, and cultural practices do not — those have real mechanical consequences and belong in the agent layer.

### 7. No eras
Civilizational development is a capability graph, not a sequence of stages. What an observer might call an era is just a summary of where a group sits in that graph. Development is shaped by environment, pressure, contact, and contingency. Two groups can reach similar practical capability via completely different paths and be deeply incomparable. Regression is possible. No transition is guaranteed.

### 8. Astronomy is terrain
The sky is a physical layer like any other. Celestial events are deterministic and scheduled from world generation. Astronomical knowledge is the capability to build accurate predictive models of what is already there. A civilization without that capability still experiences eclipses and meteor strikes — they just can't predict or explain them.

### 9. The world at genesis is purely natural
The player has not yet acted. Agents have supernatural interpretive frameworks built from cognitive bias, not from evidence. Whether the world ever becomes genuinely supernatural depends entirely on whether and how the player acts. A no-intervention run produces a civilization that builds elaborate theology around a world with no actual supernatural content.

### 10. Player intervention style shapes epistemic culture
Overt intervention produces specific theology and high certainty. Subtle intervention through plausible natural channels produces diffuse animism or possible naturalism. Long absence after overt intervention produces mythologization, institutional calcification, and interpretive drift the player cannot fully control upon return. The player is epistemically responsible for the theological culture that emerges — not through design but through the honest accumulation of their behavioral history.

### 11. Agent lifecycle follows human lifecycle
Named agents are born, live, age, and die. They are simulated during their life at a tier determined by their relevance. After death they become history — immutable archived state that persists as the player's omniscient record, referenced by the cultural memory system but never modified. There is no special treatment for important figures. The simulation tracks them the same way human civilization tracks its important figures: through memory, record, and the slow work of transmission.

### 12. Agents know only what was transmitted to them
The articulation layer has one source of truth per agent: what that agent knows. Agents describing historical figures describe what their cultural memory contains — not what the archive says. The gap between archived truth and living cultural memory is the player's to observe. It is not accessible to agents. That gap is mythology, and it is a natural output of the cultural transmission system doing its job honestly, not a simulation artifact.
