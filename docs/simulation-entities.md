# Simulation Entities

## Human Aspects — Deterministic State

These are the core encoded systems that drive all emergent behavior. They are deeply entangled — changes in one propagate through others. Detail to be refined as the capability graph develops.

### Needs
What agents require to function. Decay over time, drive action selection.

- **Physiological** — food, water, sleep, shelter, warmth
- **Safety** — physical security, predictability, stability
- **Social** — belonging, status, intimacy
- **Meaning** — purpose, explanation, transcendence

Meaning is the substrate from which religion, ideology, art, and philosophy emerge. It is a genuine need, not a luxury.

### Traits
Stable individual characteristics that influence utility weights and behavior tendencies. Inherited and mutated across generations.

- **Dispositional** — brave, cautious, aggressive, empathetic
- **Intellectual** — curious, credulous, contrarian
- **Social** — charismatic, deferential, dominant
- **Moral** — compassionate, tribal, justice-seeking

### Relationships
Connections between agents that update based on experience.

- Kinship, friendship, rivalry, romantic bonds, hierarchy
- Trust levels that update dynamically
- Collective identities: family, clan, tribe, institution

### Memory
What agents carry forward through time.

- Personal experiences with emotional weight
- Inherited cultural memory with epistemological decay over generations
- Traumatic events that reshape trait expression

### Interactions between systems
These systems are not independent. Illustrative examples:
- Traumatic memory → elevated threat-sensitivity → altered social behavior → changed relationship formation → changed cultural transmission
- Unmet meaning need → receptivity to religious frameworks → trait drift toward piety → influence on children's inherited traits

Full interaction map to be detailed as the capability graph develops.

## Genesis Capability Set

Simulation begins approximately 300,000 years ago with anatomically modern *Homo sapiens* in Africa. "Became human" is a gradient not a moment — the starting package reflects the cognitive and behavioral consensus for early modern humans, acknowledging genuine uncertainty where it exists.

### Cognitive foundations
These are not nodes in the capability graph — they are the prerequisites for the graph to function at all. Every agent starts with:

- **Language** — complex communication, likely not fully modern syntax but sufficient for abstract thought and cultural transmission
- **Theory of mind** — understanding other agents have beliefs, intentions, and knowledge states different from one's own
- **Causal reasoning** — inferring hidden causes from observable effects; the cognitive root of both science and religion
- **Cumulative culture** — learning from others and building on it across generations; what makes the capability graph possible
- **Planning depth** — thinking and coordinating beyond the immediate present

### Starting capability nodes

**Practical**
- Fire control and maintenance — not necessarily independent ignition
- Knapping — producing sharp tools from stone
- Foraging knowledge — plants, seasons, locations, animal behavior patterns
- Opportunistic shelter — using terrain, caves, and available materials
- Rudimentary wound treatment — cleaning, herb application

**Social**
- Kinship recognition and tracking
- Status hierarchy formation and maintenance
- Coalition building
- Reciprocal exchange — proto-trade and gift economy
- Collective ritual — evidence suggests this is among the earliest behaviors, possibly predating full modern cognition

**Proto-religious**
This is not a later development — it is present at genesis. The cognitive architecture that produces ritual, taboo, and supernatural attribution appears inseparable from the same architecture that produces language and causal reasoning. Starting agents will already interpret unexplained phenomena as agentive, already perform ritual, already maintain taboo.

Humans are hyperactive agency detectors — we evolved to over-attribute agency because falsely assuming a predator is present is far less costly than falsely assuming it isn't. Agents therefore start with animism: a diffuse supernatural worldview built entirely from cognitive bias applied to a purely natural world. Thunder is someone. The river wants something. Disease is punishment from an unspecified force. None of this reflects actual supernatural causation at genesis — it reflects how humans are wired to interpret natural events.

**The world at genesis is purely natural.** The player has not yet acted. Animism is not a rational response to actual supernatural phenomena — it is cognitive architecture predating any evidence. Whether the world ever becomes genuinely supernatural depends entirely on whether the player acts.

**Agents start with no knowledge of the player as a specific entity.** The player is not known at genesis. They are discovered — or never discovered at all.

**Rationalism is not guaranteed.** Without player intervention the world remains entirely natural. Whether agents ever develop naturalistic causal reasoning as a dominant mode depends on population pressure, cognitive surplus, institutional conditions, and contingency. A civilization can reach high practical capability while never developing anything resembling the scientific method. This is historically plausible and the sim should reflect it.

### What is genuinely uncertain
Symbolic thought — ochre use and shell beads appear 75,000-100,000 years ago but whether earlier humans had symbolic capacity without leaving durable material evidence is unknown. The simulation should treat symbolic capability as present but undeveloped at genesis, discoverable early rather than unlocked at a fixed point.

### What is not present at genesis
- Independent fire starting
- Constructed shelter
- Pottery
- Agriculture
- Any form of writing or durable record
- Metal use
- Formal political structures beyond band-level hierarchy

These are all reachable from the starting graph given the right conditions — population pressure, environmental context, cognitive surplus, and time.

## Knowledge & Epistemology

Knowledge is a first-class entity in the simulation. It is not a technology tree — it is an epistemological state held individually and collectively, which can be correct, incorrect, or partially correct, and which updates through observation, experimentation, and cultural transmission.

### Knowledge vs Understanding
These are distinct states an agent can hold:

- **Ignorance** — the agent has no proposition about a phenomenon
- **Misattribution** — the agent has a wrong causal model (illness from divine punishment, not pathogens)
- **Rule without understanding** — the agent follows a correct behavior without knowing why (avoid the sick because God said so)
- **Partial understanding** — correct directionally, wrong in mechanism
- **Full understanding** — accurate causal model with correct implications

Most agents hold a mix of these across different domains.

### Divine Intervention and Knowledge Transfer

**Revelation** — God communicates a proposition. The agent holds a new belief but lacks the conceptual infrastructure to fully operationalize it. Consequences:
- Partial or cargo-culted compliance — the rule is followed without its implications
- Generational drift — descendants follow the rule as ritual, losing the original proposition
- Misapplication — the rule is applied to wrong situations without understanding to correct it

**Gnosis** — God directly instantiates understanding. The agent has the full conceptual scaffold, not just the fact. Consequences:
- Complete behavioral change with correct implications
- Severe social disruption — anachronistic knowledge is incomprehensible to peers
- Prophet or deviant status depending on community traits and event spectacularity
- Knowledge may die with the agent if the community lacks capacity to receive it

### The civilizational dependency trap
A civilization that receives knowledge as divine gift rather than earning it through inquiry may never develop the epistemological capacity to generate knowledge independently. They become structurally dependent — every hard problem requires waiting for God. This fragility only becomes visible centuries later when a crisis arrives that God does not address.

This is an emergent consequence, not a game mechanic. The player will feel it without being warned.

### Knowledge transmission
- Oral tradition — fast but lossy, mutates across generations
- Written record — slower to spread but resistant to drift
- Institutional transmission — formalized but vulnerable to political distortion
- Direct observation — high fidelity but limited reach

### The player's epistemic dilemma
The player is omniscient. Agents are not. The player watches agents die from ignorance of germ theory, make catastrophic decisions from misattributed causality, and follow cargo-culted rules that drift from their original purpose. The player can intervene via revelation or gnosis — but both have real consequences that compound across generations. Choosing not to intervene is also a choice.
