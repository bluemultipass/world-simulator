# Civilizational Scope

The simulation spans prehistory to modernity but has no eras. Eras are an abstraction imposed on history for human comprehension — real civilizations accumulate capabilities unevenly, lose them, rediscover them, and develop in directions that don't fit neat categories.

Instead, civilizational development is modeled as a capability graph. What an observer might call an "era" is just a summary of where a group sits in that graph at a given moment.

---

## Capability Graph

Each node is a discrete understanding or technique. Edges are dependencies.

Every knowledge node has:
- **Prerequisites** — what must already be known
- **Discovery conditions** — what observations, pressures, or circumstances make it discoverable
- **Transmission requirements** — what infrastructure is needed to pass it on reliably
- **Loss conditions** — what causes it to be forgotten

Discovery conditions are not random. Agriculture becomes discoverable when a group has sufficient population pressure on food supply, sufficient settlement stability to observe plant growth cycles, and sufficient cognitive surplus to experiment. The physical and social conditions have to be right.

### What this produces naturally

- A tribe near copper deposits discovers metallurgy early but never develops writing
- A tribe with gnosis-gifted agricultural knowledge skips hunting entirely but has no metallurgy
- A tribe that suffers epidemic loses knowledge that wasn't written down — genuine technological regression
- Two groups at similar apparent capability levels are actually incomparable because their graphs look completely different
- No tribe follows the same path — development is shaped by environment, pressure, contact, and contingency

### Granularity principle

The graph should be granular enough that two groups can be interestingly incomparable, coarse enough to be manageable. The target is meaningful asymmetry, not exhaustive modeling of every subspecialty.

## Persistent Identity Across Development
- **Lineage** — agents die and have children; descendants of originals are tracked
- **Cultural memory** — beliefs, myths, and traditions evolve but persist (e.g. a river god worshipped because Rowan prayed there in tick 25)
- **Institutional continuity** — tribe → village → city retains identity regardless of capability level
- **Knowledge loss** — regression is possible and historically honest; a destroyed settlement can take irreplaceable knowledge with it
